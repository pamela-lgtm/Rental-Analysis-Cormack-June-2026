When the listing won't sell

Yes — you don't want to be a landlord. Let's do the math anyway.

Nobody lists their home for sale hoping to rent it out. But if it's been sitting — drawing few showings, or showings but no offers — the market is already telling you something, and every month you wait carries a number you never get back. Before you cut the price again, look at what waiting actually costs.

The real cost is below

First, the uncomfortable part

A listing that sits isn't bad luck. It's the market quoting you a price — and waiting for a different answer isn't free.

When a well-kept home draws showings but no offers, the issue usually isn't the home. It's the gap between the asking price and what today's buyers will actually pay. You can close that gap one of two ways: cut the price until a buyer feels they're getting a deal, or keep paying to hold while you wait for the market to come to you.

Both cost real money. The price cut is a number you can see. The holding cost is the quieter one — taxes, insurance, HOA, utilities, and a mortgage that doesn't pause — draining out month after month with nothing coming in. That money is gone for good. It is never recouped at closing, no matter what the home eventually sells for.

The question to actually ask

Don't ask whether the rent is "enough." Ask what an empty month costs.

It's natural to look at a rent figure and think "that won't be enough." But the rent isn't competing with your wish price — it's competing with zero. Every month the home sits, the full carrying cost walks out the door and never comes back. The rent has one job: stop that bleed. Measured against an empty house rather than a number in your head, even a solid market rent wins decisively — which is exactly why the smart move is to lease quickly, not to hold out for a few more dollars a month.

The honest answers

Two worries talk most owners out of the smart move. Let's take them head-on.

"The rent won't even cover my mortgage."

It's the most common reason owners talk themselves out of renting — and it quietly confuses two different things. Your mortgage and the rent were never meant to match dollar-for-dollar. What actually matters is how much of your monthly outflow stops coming out of your pocket.

Even partial coverage changes the whole picture — and a portion of every payment is paying down your principal. That isn't money spent; it's equity you keep. A vacant home, by contrast, is 100% loss with nothing building behind it.

65–70% Cover even two-thirds of your costs and that's two-thirds you stop paying out of pocket — with the principal portion landing in your equity. A forced savings account, not a bill.
"What if we get nightmare tenants who wreck it?"

Start by separating two things people lump together: damage and wear. Real damage is rare — especially when you partner with the right management team — and it's exactly what screening, the deposit, and the lease are built to cover. Normal wear is just what happens when people live somewhere.

And here's the honest part: a house ages whether anyone lives in it or not — that's simply what a physical thing does. So the real choice isn't "lived-in versus untouched." It's "occupied by the right resident and earning, versus sitting empty, aging, and earning nothing."

This is where the product matters. A premium, show-ready home — fresh, neutrally painted walls (think soft greige, a grayish-beige), pristinely kept, especially in Lake Nona — draws a premium resident to match it.
